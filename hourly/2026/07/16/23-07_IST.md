## Meeting language

| Situation | Credible formulation |
|---|---|
| A new benchmark is proposed as decisive evidence | “Before changing our architecture, we should verify the benchmark’s publication date, evidence-access conditions, scaffold dependence, task realism, and reproducibility.” |
| A popularity trend is presented as adoption evidence | “Repository stars and model downloads are discovery signals; production adoption requires sustained usage, integration evidence, maintenance activity, and measurable workload value.” |
| An older paper resurfaces | “The work may still be useful, but we should classify it as background unless it changes a current decision or has new independent validation.” |

## Claims to avoid

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| “This benchmark proves the best agent.” | “This benchmark ranks the evaluated model-and-scaffold combinations under its stated tasks, budgets, and evidence conditions.” |
| “Trending on GitHub means industry adoption.” | “GitHub momentum is an attention signal that requires longitudinal maintenance and implementation evidence.” |
| “More evaluation tasks always produce better decisions.” | “Evaluation breadth should be balanced against rank stability, task representativeness, and cost.” |
